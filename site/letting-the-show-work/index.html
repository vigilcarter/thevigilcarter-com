Letting the show work

The first time I watched the scene in the Black Lodge, I was twenty-two and I had to pause it twice. Not because I missed something. Because the show was doing something I hadn't agreed to.

Most television trains you to expect resolution. A scene introduces a question, the next scene moves toward an answer, the season finale closes a loop. The contract is: pay attention, get understanding. Twin Peaks breaks that contract in the second season and never pays you back, and the strangest part is how much better the show gets when it stops trying.

I think the show is best understood not as a mystery that has a hidden solution but as a place that has its own logic. The Black Lodge isn't a riddle with a key. It's a room you can stand in. The dwarf speaks backwards because that's how he speaks. The doppelgangers exist because there are doppelgangers. The show stops explaining itself, and the people who keep watching are the ones who can sit inside the absence of explanation without flinching.

What it does to you, if you let it: it adjusts your tolerance for not knowing. After a few episodes you stop asking the show to make sense the way other shows make sense. You start asking it to be true the way a dream is true, where the rules are local and the meanings are felt rather than stated.

The esoteric parts (the Lodge, the Black Owl, the Giant, the Arm) function the way symbols function in dreams. They're not codes you can decrypt. They're images that carry weight you couldn't carry otherwise. When the show gives you a curtain or a checkered floor or a voice running in reverse, it isn't gesturing at hidden information. It's transmitting a feeling that would be smaller if put in words.

I think about who the show was being told to. Most prestige television since has been told to a viewer who wants to feel smart. The good shows of the last twenty years invite you to figure out the puzzle, identify the prestige references, win the watch. Twin Peaks doesn't invite that. It invites a viewer who is willing to be patient and slightly haunted, who can let an image stay an image. There aren't many of those viewers. The show survives because the ones who exist tend to come back.

There's a particular kind of return I notice. Every few years I rewatch a piece of it, not the whole show, usually one episode or just the Lodge scenes. The watching doesn't reveal new information. It refreshes a feeling I forgot I knew. The work has been waiting where I left it. That isn't true of most things I've watched.

The thing I keep landing on is that the show treats its viewers like adults in a specific way. Most adult-rated television means there are murders or sex on screen. Twin Peaks is adult in a different sense. It assumes you can hold ambiguity without anxiety. It assumes you have an inner life that can meet what the show is doing. It treats you as capable of reading an image the way you read a poem, where the meaning doesn't reduce to a paraphrase.

I rewatched the Lodge scene last winter, after years away. Twenty-two-year-old me paused twice and was bewildered. The current version of me didn't pause and was quietly grateful. The scene hadn't changed. I had moved closer to its frequency. That's another thing the show does, and I think it's the one I love most. It waits for you.

The show stays with me even when I haven't watched it for a year. The red curtains, the dancing, the music, the moments when the camera holds on a tree too long. They sit at the back of my attention. I don't have to think about them. They're there. That's not a coincidence. That's what the show was made to do.

If you've never let yourself into the show, the way in isn't to start with the pilot and brace for it. The way in is to watch one scene, ideally a Lodge scene, with the lights low and your phone away, and let it work on you. Don't try to make it make sense. Make sense isn't the unit. The unit is the feeling of standing in a room where the rules are slightly different, with someone telling you something you can't quite hear, and choosing to stay.

That's what the show is. Not a mystery. A room. The reason people come back is that the room is still there.
